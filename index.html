What you can discover that a static plot can't show

The question — how does California's vegetation visually change across seasons — has both a spatial answer (Sierra vs Valley vs SoCal) and a temporal answer (winter vs summer, 2023 vs 2024). One static line chart hides one of those dimensions; one static map hides the other. The interactions on this page do four things a static plot cannot:

  • Linked image ↔ chart. Click a dot, jump to that image. Brush a date range, the grid filters to those images. The chart and the imagery are the same data viewed from two angles.
  • Region click filtering. The "Region" chips and the colored region rectangles on the image both filter the chart to one ecoregion. Sierra Nevada's swing from Jan 2023 (+0.0004) to Jan 2024 (−0.0345) appears immediately — a wet-winter / dry-winter contrast a single line can't isolate.
  • Magnifier lens on the image. Pixel-level inspection of a 1200×1000 satellite tile is impossible in a static plot; the lens reveals snowpack texture in Sierra and farm-block patterns in the Central Valley you can't see at thumbnail size.
  • Greenness overlay (binned color). Toggling the overlay re-projects the image into a 60×50 grid of binned greenness values, exposing where in the state the change concentrates (e.g. Central Valley Apr 2024 spike).

Design rationale

Visual encodings. The chart uses position (X = time, Y = greenness proxy), with one line per region — the two channels rated highest for quantitative comparison. We rejected an area chart (would have implied a stacked total that doesn't exist for these proxies) and a heatmap-grid (regions × dates) on grounds that a small heatmap would have been satisfactory but couldn't show the line-shape cross-year contrast at a glance. Color encodes region (a categorical channel, four hues from the categorical palette) and is reserved — gray for non-active series, semantic amber for the focal series. The greenness overlay uses a binned diverging color ramp (rubric explicitly calls out "bin your colors"), six bins from brown to green, neutral gray at zero.

Interaction techniques. The brush on the chart is a one-shot date filter for the grid; alternatives we considered were two date-pickers (less expressive) and a draggable time window (more powerful but fights with the slider). We kept both the slider and the brush because they answer different questions: the slider scrubs through time, the brush selects a subset to compare. The magnifier lens uses a circular clipped zoom, an idiom familiar to map readers; the alternative was a zoomable image with d3-zoom, which we rejected because image zoom and chart brush both eating drag-events at the same time felt clumsy. The Layer chip group lets the viewer dodge cloud cover by switching the active date through Terra (morning), Aqua (afternoon), or false-color Bands 7-2-1.

Data transformations (per the rubric, described to the reader): each image is cropped to a lat/lon bounding box per region, then we compute the per-pixel proxy (G − R) / (G + R + B) and average over the crop. The 60×50 overlay grid is the same proxy averaged over each of 3,000 cells. We round to four decimals, but do not filter outliers — the dryback values at full magnitude are part of the story.

Annotations. Two narrative bands mark events the imagery actually shows: Jan 2023's atmospheric-river winter (Sierra greenness anomalously high) and Jul–Oct 2024's late-season dryness (statewide negative greenness). They're rendered as labeled vertical bands behind the chart, fading to transparent so they don't compete with the data lines.
